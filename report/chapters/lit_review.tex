\section{Image Segmentation and K-Means Clustering}

Before one can do any processing on an image, it's wise if one breaks it into
pieces that actually mean something. This is what segmentation
is.
\cite{shi2000}.

We picked K-Means for this because it requires zero labelled training data and the math
is straightforward enough to implement from scratch. One gives it
a number $K$ and it finds $K$ colour groups on its own by minimising how spread out
each group is internally:

\begin{equation}
    J = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \sum_{\mathbf{x} \in C_i} \|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i\|^2
\end{equation}

\noindent Here $C_i$ is the $i$-th cluster and $\boldsymbol{\mu}_i$ is its centroid. Every pixel picks the closest centroid, centroids move to the average of
their new members, repeat until nothing changes \cite{jain_kmeans_survey}. For
colour-based tasks it holds up well even against fancier methods \cite{comaniciu2002}.

\vspace{0.15in}
\noindent
The problem is that K-Means draws sharp, blocky lines between clusters \cite{jain_kmeans_survey}. There is no gradual blending,
just a hard cut. Those artificial boundaries turned out to be a real problem for our
pipeline, which is exactly why we brought Inverse DCT in as a diagnostic tool later on.

\section{The Discrete Fourier Transform}

The DFT is what lets one look at an image from a completely different perspective. Instead of
a grid of colour values, they sees a map of how much of each frequency is present in the
image \cite{oppenheim1999}. The 2D version for an image $f(x, y)$ of size $M \times N$
looks like this:

\begin{equation}
    F(u, v) = \sum_{x=0}^{M-1} \sum_{y=0}^{N-1} f(x, y) \cdot
    e^{-j 2\pi \left(\frac{ux}{M} + \frac{vy}{N}\right)}
\end{equation}

\noindent
The result is a grid of complex numbers, which is useless to look at directly.
To actually see anything useful, you find the magnitude:

\begin{equation}
    |F(u,v)| = \sqrt{\operatorname{Re}(F)^2 + \operatorname{Im}(F)^2}
\end{equation}

Even then, the first time we plotted this it was just a black screen with a
white dot in the centre. The issue is the dynamic range: the DC component (the
average brightness of the whole image) is millions of times larger than the high
frequency values.


\vspace{0.15in}
\noindent
Once we are in the frequency domain, filtering is just multiplication.
We built a mask $H(u,v)$, multiplied it with $F(u,v)$, and transform back with the IDFT
\cite{brigham1988}. The one thing we learned the hard way is that using a hard circular
cutoff for the mask creates undesirable ringing in the output, which is the Gibbs phenomenon
\cite{pratt2007}. Switching to Gaussian and Butterworth masks, which fade out gradually
instead of cutting off sharply, fixed it.


\section{The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)}

Even though DFT provide the theoretical basis for frequency analysis, computing it using the standard double-summation formula is computationally expensive, with a complexity of $O(N^4)$ for an $N \times N$ image. For any practical real-time application, this is extremely slow. To solve this, we used the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), the Cooley-Tukey algorithm \cite{cooley1965}.

\vspace{0.15in}
\noindent
The FFT uses the periodicity and symmetry of the complex exponential (twiddle factors) to recursively break down a DFT of size $N$ into smaller DFTs. This reduces the computational complexity to:

\begin{equation}
    O(N^2 \log N)
\end{equation}


\vspace{0.15in}
\noindent
One of the hurdles we had encountered was that the most efficient radix-2 FFT algorithms require the input image dimensions to be a power of two ($2^n$). Since our source images were of rectangular resolutions, we had to implement zero-padding to the next power of two before processing.


\section{The Discrete Cosine Transform}

The DCT does something similar to the DFT but uses only real cosine functions instead
of complex exponentials, which makes it much cheaper to compute and easier to work
with in C++ \cite{ahmed1974}. The 2D form we implemented is:

\begin{equation}
    C(u, v) = \alpha_u \alpha_v \sum_{x=0}^{M-1} \sum_{y=0}^{N-1}
    f(x, y) \cdot \cos\!\left[\frac{\pi(2x+1)u}{2M}\right]
    \cos\!\left[\frac{\pi(2y+1)v}{2N}\right]
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\alpha_0 = \sqrt{1/M}$ and $\alpha_u = \sqrt{2/M}$ for $u > 0$
(same idea for $v$).

\vspace{0.15in}
\noindent
DCT is extremely good at summarizing almost all of an
image's meaningful information into the top-left corner of the frequency matrix. When we ran
DCT on a K-Means segmented image, the artificial boundaries from the clustering lit up
the spectrum immediately. That gave us a direct way to measure how much damage the
segmentation was doing and target those specific artefacts for removal.
